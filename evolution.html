Evolving  Algorithms

Part1. Algorithmic Megalomania

Following the idea for the initial watch-next algorithm Flinch continued to expand his dataset from the fruits of wee Albert's after-movie notebook scribblings. He began a successful pampleting campaign on the release of each new movie at the Troxie. He would meticulously scan his dataset for potential viewer matches. Then he would mail out VIP-style invitations.

Flinch's Algorithm worked! Movie audiences felt valued. And the Troxie began - and continued - to see significant year-on-year revenue growth through the second half of the 1950s. This sparked great interest from news outlets. Not all commentators were sympathetic to Flinch's aims or methods. Reportedly, Flinch's discovery was also sparking great envy among his competitors.

1958 saw the peak of the algorithm's success Coinciding with it, Flinch was accused of heinous misdoings when three members of the local gentlemen's rotary fellowship were wrongly petitioned to come and view a risque, uncensored foreign movie.

Flinch protested the pamphlets were not of his doing and that the reported under-the-counter movie never had any planned screenings at the Troxie. But the flames of the story were fanned by Flinch's critics. Those flames could not be extinguished.

The resulting community inferno was catalysed the more once the newspaper front-paged the story. Many readers, previously unaware, realised that they themselves were potentially being profiled through Flinch's stub-retention algorithm. On that revelation, uproar turned to anger.

Anarchic placard protestors gathered regularly at the Troxie, harassing and attempting to intimidate movie attendees while demanding Flinch shelve his algorithm. Flinch though, was not only unrepentant but was increasingly hostile.

At the same time it was reported that Flinch was subject to threats on himself and his property. Many locals were angry at the profiling. Curiously though, many seemed unconcerned with the concept of being reduced to data. As a result, the Troxie continued to profit.

Competitors on the other hand became increasingly provoked as their premises underwent dramatic decline. Though never substantiated, it was claimed that a plot existed to put Flinch out of business...

But in December 1959 accumulated local anger turned to righteous indignation after revelations that Flinch had not only been paying wee Albert in the dregs from spent Buckfast bottles (a poteen brand locally outlawed at the time) but also that Albert had himself developed a permanent stoop from collecting stubs off of the Troxie steps.

New Year's Eve as the decade ended, the Troxie burned to the ground. The Fire Station Chief declared the cause of the blaze to be a firework. Arson was suspected. It was never proven. The building and Flinch's algorithmic records were destroyed.

Wee Albert went missing It was widely rumoured that Albert had absconded with the core component of Flinch's algorithm: the notebook dataset.

As the new decade began Albert was never heard from again.

Part2. Gangsters of the Algorithm

Realising the lucrative potential of good watch-next algorithms prompted the great algorithmic accuracy rush of the 1960s. It was and part-time movie mogul Geordie Haddock of King George Studios that spearheaded advanced algorithmic watch-next research.
